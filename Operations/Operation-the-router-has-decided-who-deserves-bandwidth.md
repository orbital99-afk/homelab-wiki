# Operation The Router Has Decided Who Deserves Bandwidth

## A Practical Guide to Turning Half Your Internet Speed Into Thermodynamics

> **Status:** Completed

## Objective

Determine why internet performance fell after enabling a feature specifically advertised as improving internet performance.

The practical objectives were simple, if emotionally exhausting:

- confirm the slowdown was real
- compare behaviour with QoS enabled and disabled
- determine whether the ISP, devices, Wi-Fi, cabling, or router were responsible
- identify whether traffic classification or packet processing imposed a throughput penalty
- restore expected internet performance
- document the irony for future generations

The central question, as always, was not whether the router could do something. The central question was whether it should have done it in the first place.

> “How did a feature designed to improve internet speed successfully make the internet slower?”

## Background

QoS sounded sensible.

It promised:

- prioritisation
- better responsiveness
- smoother gaming
- improved streaming
- fair traffic handling
- less congestion

The Committee therefore expected:

- faster-feeling internet
- fewer interruptions
- more intelligent bandwidth use

What it received was:

- less speed
- more processing
- a warmer router
- and a feature confidently solving a problem by creating a larger one

The irony repeated itself in several forms:

- the optimisation layer became the bottleneck
- the system improved fairness by ensuring everyone received less
- the router prioritised traffic so thoroughly that forwarding it became optional
- the internet was organised, classified, stamped, and delayed
- every packet received excellent administrative service and poor transportation service

The core finding was therefore both simple and unacceptable:

> QoS did not lose the bandwidth. It processed it into paperwork.

## Phase 1: The Promise

The decision to enable QoS was treated as the launch of a major public infrastructure programme. The Committee had been promised a better, more efficient network experience and had accepted the proposal with the suspicionless optimism of people who had never before been disappointed by a feature name.

The official public-facing case for QoS was that packets would be prioritised, latency would improve, streaming would become smoother, gaming traffic would receive diplomatic immunity, important packets would use the express lane, and less important packets would wait politely.

Meanwhile, the actual internal committee briefing was less glamorous and more alarming:

- important traffic would be given a title
- less important traffic would be invited to queue
- the router would become an administrator with a strong opinion about packet status
- “quality” would be defined in a way that felt expensive and unnecessary

The Committee approved the feature because “Quality of Service” sounded significantly better than “Router Makes Decisions About Your Traffic.”

## Phase 2: The Slowdown

The slowdown was discovered in the ordinary way: by noticing that the internet had become worse without obvious cause.

Symptoms included:

- lower throughput
- speed tests underperforming
- downloads taking longer
- the internet feeling constrained
- the router working harder to deliver less

The Committee was not pleased. The router, however, appeared to be enjoying the new level of responsibility.

The effect was not subtle. The connection felt as if it had been put on an administrative diet.

The router had successfully reduced congestion by reducing traffic.

## Phase 3: Suspect Board

The incident required a suspect board, because the Committee believed every problem should be accompanied by a wall of paper and a second wall of opinions.

### Reasonable Suspects

These were the ordinary, credible explanations that the Committee briefly considered before becoming theatrical:

- ISP performance
- Wi-Fi interference
- Ethernet negotiation
- device limitations
- background downloads
- Plex activity
- cloud backups
- VPN overhead
- router CPU load
- QoS packet inspection and shaping
- misconfigured bandwidth limits

### Committee-Approved Suspects

The absurd hypotheses were numerous and, in this case, mostly justified by the behaviour of the feature:

- packets had been assigned social classes
- premium packets were using the executive lounge
- ordinary packets were awaiting committee approval
- the router had nationalised the bandwidth
- half the internet speed had been converted into heat
- ASUS had invented packet bureaucracy
- gaming packets were receiving police escorts
- streaming packets were being searched at customs
- download packets had failed a means test
- the router had become philosophically opposed to unrestricted throughput
- Fusco was hoarding megabits in a hidden share
- Reese was quietly consuming bandwidth to maintain aluminium-chassis morale
- Bear was storing unused bandwidth as emergency electricity
- the ISP had delivered all the speed but QoS was filing it alphabetically
- the missing megabits had entered the thermal economy
- each packet required three signatures and a coloured form before forwarding
- the router had mistaken “quality” for “quantity reduction”
- traffic shaping had become traffic sculpture

These were jokes. They were also, in a sense, accurate descriptions of the behaviour.

## Phase 4: The Controlled Experiment

The practical investigation was straightforward: test the connection with QoS enabled, disable QoS, test again under similar conditions, and observe whether the slowdown followed the setting.

The logic was simple enough for a reasonable person to follow:

- enable QoS
- observe the network behaviour
- disable QoS
- retest under comparable conditions
- compare the results
- determine whether throughput returns
- confirm the slowdown tracks the QoS state rather than the ISP

The key diagnostic principle was this:

If the connection becomes faster when the optimisation feature is disabled, the optimisation feature is no longer the optimisation feature.

## How to Turn Half Your Internet Speed Into Thermodynamics

This section is provided for the benefit of future operators who believe a feature should be trusted simply because it has a polished name and a visible toggle.

### Step 1: Purchase fast internet

Acquire a connection capable of respectable performance.

### Step 2: Decide it needs improving

Ignore evidence that the connection is already working and proceed on the assumption that the network exists for the purpose of being upgraded by a menu option.

### Step 3: Enable QoS

Activate packet inspection, prioritisation, shaping, classification, and administrative overhead.

### Step 4: Inform the router that every packet requires a performance review

Ensure no packet can simply travel without first being assessed for importance.

### Step 5: Run a speed test

Observe that the internet is now slower but significantly more organised.

### Step 6: Place a hand near the router

Confirm that some portion of the missing confidence appears to have entered the thermal environment.

### Step 7: Disable QoS

Relieve the router of its traffic-management responsibilities.

### Step 8: Retest

Watch the internet recover immediately after being freed from governance.

### Step 9: Update The Library

Record the discovery that performance features may consume performance.

A suitable equation is therefore recommended:

Internet Speed ÷ 2 = Router Heat + Packet Administration + Disappointment

> Not recognised by conventional physics. Repeatedly supported by Committee observation.

## The Packet Class System

The QoS configuration appeared to establish an internal social hierarchy among data, and the packets responded with the passive aggression of a bureaucratic caste system.

### First-Class Packets

- gaming traffic
- selected real-time applications
- packets with lounge access
- packets travelling with priority documentation

### Business-Class Packets

- streaming traffic
- video calls
- packets permitted one checked bag

### Economy Packets

- downloads
- updates
- cloud sync
- anything Finch actually wanted at full speed

### Standby Packets

- traffic still awaiting classification
- packets delayed because the router was busy classifying packets
- packets that missed their connection due to QoS

The joke was that every class arrived later than before.

## Technical Root Cause

The router’s QoS processing introduced enough overhead or throughput limitation to reduce internet performance under the observed conditions.

Possible contributing mechanisms may include:

- traffic classification
- packet inspection
- shaping
- bandwidth limits
- router CPU constraints
- hardware acceleration being unavailable or reduced while QoS was active

No specific mechanism was asserted beyond the observed effect. The operational root cause was clear enough:

A feature intended to manage traffic became the limiting factor for traffic.

## Actions Taken

1. Confirmed the speed reduction.
2. Reviewed likely network causes.
3. Compared performance with QoS enabled and disabled.
4. Identified QoS as the setting associated with the slowdown.
5. Disabled or adjusted QoS.
6. Retested internet performance.
7. Confirmed improvement.
8. Recorded the incident.
9. Added QoS to the list of settings that require evidence before trust.

## Validation

The following were verified as part of the operation:

- throughput improved after QoS was disabled or adjusted
- the connection behaved more normally
- the slowdown tracked the QoS state
- no ISP fault was required to explain the result
- the router continued functioning
- packets resumed travelling without a full administrative hearing

## Outcome

- expected internet performance returned
- QoS was removed from active network government
- the router stopped overmanaging traffic
- the Committee learned that optimisation features can become bottlenecks
- future QoS changes require before-and-after testing
- no packets were permanently disadvantaged
- the missing bandwidth was recovered from the Department of Unnecessary Processing

## Official Findings

The Committee concluded that QoS had been enabled to improve network performance, that network performance became worse, and that disabling QoS improved performance. The optimisation layer had become the limiting factor. The router was not malicious. The router was simply overworked. The system gave excellent attention to packet priority and insufficient attention to packet velocity. Half the speed was not literally converted to heat. The Committee nevertheless considers the thermodynamics explanation emotionally accurate.

## Casualties

- several speed tests
- trust in feature names

- Root’s initial defence of the feature

- no actual violation of thermodynamics

## Lessons Learned

- benchmark before and after enabling QoS
- measure throughput and latency separately
- feature names are not performance evidence
- traffic shaping has overhead
- router CPU limits matter
- acceleration features may be affected by QoS
- disable suspected features during troubleshooting
- change one setting at a time
- record the baseline
- do not blame the ISP before testing the router
- optimisation should be proven, not assumed
- the router cannot improve speed by holding meetings about packets
- traffic classification is not traffic forwarding
- better organisation is not always better performance
- QoS can make every packet equally disappointed
- the fastest packet is the one not waiting for approval
- half the internet speed may be recoverable by abolishing packet bureaucracy
- a warm router is not proof of productive work
- the phrase “Quality of Service” contains no legally binding promise of speed
- the Committee now requires all optimisation features to demonstrate actual optimisation
- thermodynamics is where failed performance claims go to retire
